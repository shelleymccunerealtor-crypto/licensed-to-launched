📋 Choosing a Broker to Sponsor Your Exam

Before you can take the Ohio PSI exam, you must have a sponsoring broker. Here's what you need to know.
What "Sponsoring Broker" Means

Ohio law requires that a licensed salesperson work under the supervision of a licensed broker. Before ODRE will issue your license, you must list a sponsoring broker on your application. This broker is legally responsible for your activities as an agent.

⚡ Important: The broker you choose to sponsor your exam application does NOT have to be the broker you work with long-term. Many agents choose their exam sponsor and then make a more deliberate career broker decision separately — and that's perfectly fine.

What to Look for in an Exam Sponsor
✅
They are willing to sponsor you immediately
Some brokers won't sponsor you until you've already passed. Look for one who will put their name on your application before the exam — this is how Ohio works.
✅
Clear, simple agreement with no lock-in pressure
You shouldn't be signing a long-term commitment just to take your exam. Make sure you understand what you're agreeing to and that you have the ability to transfer your license later if needed.
✅
No hidden fees just for sponsorship
Exam sponsorship itself doesn't typically come with a cost, but some brokers may have startup fees. Know exactly what you're signing up for financially before you commit.
✅
They're accessible and responsive
If they're hard to reach before you join, imagine after. A sponsor who answers your pre-exam questions is a great sign of things to come.
🚩
Red Flag: Pressure to sign a long contract immediately
You should never feel rushed or pressured into a long-term commitment before you've even taken your exam. A good broker will give you time to make the right decision.
🚩
Red Flag: They can't explain their onboarding process
If a broker can't tell you clearly what happens after you pass your exam — what training looks like, what tools you'll have, who you call with questions — that's a warning sign.

🚀 Choosing a Broker for Your Career

This is the decision that will shape your first year — and potentially your entire career. Take your time. Interview at least 2–3 brokers before you commit.
The 5 Things That Actually Matter
1️⃣
Training & Mentorship — especially in Year 1
This is the #1 factor for new agents. A high commission split means nothing if you don't know how to get a client. Ask specifically: What does training look like in my first 90 days? Is there a mentor? Do I have to pay for training separately?
2️⃣
Commission Structure — understand what you actually keep
Commission splits vary wildly — 50/50, 70/30, 80/20, 100% with a cap, revenue share models, and more. Don't just look at the split — look at the caps, fees, and what you get for what you pay.
3️⃣
Tools & Technology — what's included vs. what costs extra
Ask specifically about: CRM, transaction management (Dotloop/SkySlope), marketing tools, website, lead generation. Some brokers include all of this. Others charge for everything on top of your split.
4️⃣
Culture & Support — do you feel seen here?
Real estate can be isolating. A brokerage with a strong culture — team meetings, accountability partners, broker availability, agent community — makes a massive difference in Year 1 retention.
5️⃣
Growth Potential — where can this take you?
Think beyond Year 1. Does this brokerage support agents who want to grow into teams, leadership, or multiple revenue streams? Some brokerages have a ceiling — some have a launchpad.
Red Flags to Watch For
🚩
"We'll train you on the job" with no actual plan
Vague promises about training with no structured program = you figuring it out alone. Ask for specifics. If they can't give them, walk away.
🚩
High fees + low support
Monthly desk fees, transaction fees, tech fees, marketing fees — and nothing to show for it. Calculate your real cost before you commit.
🚩
The broker is hard to reach or unavailable
In Year 1 you will have questions — sometimes urgent ones. A broker who doesn't call back or is always "too busy" will leave you stranded at the worst moments.
🚩
High agent turnover
Ask how many agents have left in the last year. A revolving door is a sign something isn't working. Happy agents stay.
📌 Shelley's Bottom Line: Interview brokers like you're hiring them — because you are. You are an independent contractor and they work for you as much as you work with them. The right broker will feel excited to have you, invest in your growth, and be available when you need them. Don't settle.

🗺️ Your Licensing Roadmap

Step-by-step: everything you need to do to get your Ohio real estate license.

👩‍💼
The path to your license is clearer than you think. 🗺️
I remember staring at the ODRE website wondering where to even start. This roadmap breaks it down into 8 simple steps — what to do, how long it takes, what it costs, and what to watch out for along the way. You've got this. — Shelley McCune, REALTOR®
1
Meet the Basic Requirements
Before you do anything else, confirm you qualify. Ohio requires you to be at least 18 years old, have a high school diploma or GED, and be a U.S. citizen or lawfully present alien. You also must be able to pass a background check — more on that in Step 3.
⏱ 1 day💰 Free
2
Complete Your 120-Hour Pre-License Education
Ohio requires 120 hours of approved pre-licensing education before you can sit for the exam. This is split into two parts:
  • Part 1 (40 hours): Real Estate Principles & Practices
  • Part 2 (40 hours): Ohio Real Estate Law (including 10 hours of civil rights law & canal lands)
  • Part 3 (40 hours): Real Estate Appraisal

Courses can be taken online, in-person, or in a hybrid format. See the Courses tab for Shelley's top picks and a side-by-side comparison.

⏱ 4–12 weeks💰 $200–$800📌 Check the Courses tab
3
Complete a Background Check
Ohio requires a BCII (Bureau of Criminal Identification & Investigation) background check as part of your license application. You'll need to get fingerprinted through a BCII-approved Webcheck location (many UPS stores, libraries, and sheriff's offices offer this). Results are sent directly to ODRE.

⚠️ Past records? Having a criminal history doesn't automatically disqualify you. ODRE evaluates on a case-by-case basis. You can submit a Request for Determination of Eligibility to ODRE before investing in courses if you have concerns. See ODRE guidance →

⏱ 1–2 weeks💰 ~$35–$70📌 Schedule ASAP — don't wait
4
Find a Sponsoring Broker
Before you can submit your exam application, you need a licensed Ohio broker who agrees to sponsor you. This broker will be listed on your exam application. You can change brokers after passing — this is just to get your application submitted.

💡 Shelley's tip: If you're planning to join eXp Realty, you can list eXp as your sponsoring broker now — and name Shelley as your Sponsor & Certified Mentor from Day 1. See the Choose a Broker tab for the full picture.

⏱ 1–2 weeks💰 Free📌 Check the Broker tab
5
Apply to Take the State Exam via ODRE
Submit your Salesperson Exam Application to the Ohio Division of Real Estate & Professional Licensing (ODRE). You'll include proof of your 120-hour course completion, background check, your sponsoring broker's info, and the application fee.

Once ODRE approves your application, you'll receive an Authorization to Test (ATT) letter — this is your ticket to schedule the exam with PSI Exams.

Submit application on ODRE website →
⏱ 2–4 weeks for approval💰 $81 application fee
6
Schedule & Pass the PSI Exam
Once you have your ATT letter, schedule your exam through PSI Exams. The Ohio real estate salesperson exam has two portions:
  • National Portion: 80 questions — you need 53/80 (66.25%) to pass
  • Ohio State Portion: 40 questions — you need 28/40 (70%) to pass

You have 1 year from your ATT date to pass both portions. Portions can be taken together or separately. If you fail one, you only need to retake that portion — a $60 retake fee applies. Use the Practice Exams in this hub to prep!

7
Activate Your License — Post-Exam Requirements
Congratulations — you passed! 🎉 Now you need to activate your license. Within 10 days of passing you (or your broker) must submit:
  • License application signed by your sponsoring broker
  • License fee: $135 for a 3-year license
  • Errors & Omissions (E&O) insurance — required before your license activates (eXp includes this)
  • Recovery Fund fee: $10 first-time applicants only

❓ Frequently Asked Questions

How long does the whole process take?▾
Most people complete the full process in 3–6 months depending on how quickly they finish their courses and how fast ODRE processes their application. If you're motivated and study consistently, some people do it in as little as 8–10 weeks.
Can I take the courses online?▾
Yes! Ohio allows fully online pre-license education. Most popular providers offer self-paced online courses so you can study on your schedule. See the Courses tab for Shelley's top picks.
What score do I need to pass the exam?▾
For the National portion: 53/80 correct (66.25%). For the Ohio State portion: 28/40 correct (70%). Shelley recommends aiming for 80%+ on practice exams so you have a comfortable cushion going in — which is what this hub's goal line is set to.
What happens if I fail the exam?▾
Don't panic — it happens to a lot of people. You can retake whichever portion(s) you failed by paying a $60 retake fee and scheduling again through PSI. You have 1 year from your ATT date to pass both portions. If you don't pass within that window, you'll need to reapply.
Do I need a broker before I take the exam?▾
Yes — Ohio requires you to have a sponsoring broker listed on your exam application before ODRE will approve it. This doesn't have to be your permanent broker. You can transfer to a different brokerage after you pass.
What is a Certified Mentor and why does it matter?▾
eXp Realty has an official Mentor Program where new agents work with a Certified Mentor on their first 3 transactions. The mentor guides you through contracts, negotiations, and the transaction process in real time. Shelley is a Certified Mentor at eXp — which means when you join with her as your Sponsor, she walks alongside you through those critical first deals. Your first 3 transactions under the Mentor Program run at a 70/30 split — you keep 70%.
How much can I realistically make in my first year?▾
This varies widely and depends entirely on your market, your effort, and your brokerage support. The NAR reports the median gross income for agents with less than 2 years of experience is around $8,800 — but that includes people who barely started. Agents who commit fully, have strong brokerage support, and work their sphere consistently can earn $40,000–$80,000+ in Year 1. There is no ceiling in this business.
When does my Ohio license expire?▾
Ohio real estate salesperson licenses are valid for 3 years and must be renewed by your renewal date. You'll need to complete 30 hours of continuing education per renewal cycle. The Ohio Division of Real Estate sends renewal notices, but it's your responsibility to renew on time.
